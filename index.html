Part 1 of 7 · The big idea

First, the one problem behind everything

Forget banking for a minute. Let's talk about something you already understand: two apps that store the same thing in different ways.

Imagine you keep your contacts in two different phone apps.

App A saves a person like this: a box called First name with "Maria" in it, and a separate box called Last name with "Garcia" in it. Two boxes.

App B is different. It has only one box, called Full name, and it expects the whole thing — "Maria Garcia" — written together.

Now you want to copy Maria from App A into App B. Simple for a human. But a computer is literal. It needs to be told, in exact words: "To fill App B's one box, take App A's first-name box, add a space, then add App A's last-name box."

That instruction — this box here becomes that box there, in this exact way — is the whole job. In the industry it is called a mapping. Everything else on this page is just bigger, more careful versions of this one idea.

Part 2 of 7 · Mapping

What "mapping" means — drawn out

Let's see Maria move from App A to App B. Watch the single rule in the middle. That rule is the mapping.

Now scale this up. A real business doesn't have 2 boxes — it has systems with hundreds of boxes, and the rules aren't always "just join with a space." Sometimes a box must be reformatted (a date written 20/06/2026 on one side must become 2026-06-20 on the other). Sometimes a box on the left has no matching box on the right, or vice versa.

Someone has to sit down and write the complete list of these rules so that no box is forgotten and nothing is mistranslated. That document is called a mapping specification ("spec" for short).

Recap: A mapping = a rule that turns a field on one side into a field on the other. A mapping specification = the full list of those rules. Writing that list is a big part of the Business Analyst's job.
Part 3 of 7 · APIs

So… what is an "API", and why is it needed?

We've been copying Maria by hand. But in a real company, the two systems must talk to each other automatically, many times a second. How? Through something called an API.

Here is the simplest way to understand it.

🍽️ Everyday picture · the restaurant

You sit at a table in a restaurant. You're hungry. The kitchen has all the food, but you are not allowed to walk into the kitchen and rummage around. That would be chaos — and dangerous.

Instead, there's a menu (a list of exactly what you're allowed to ask for) and a waiter (who takes your order to the kitchen and brings back the dish). You ask in the agreed way; you get back a known result.

An API is the menu + the waiter. It is the official, agreed doorway through which one computer system asks another for something — without being allowed to barge into its private database.

"API" stands for Application Programming Interface, but the words don't matter. What matters is the idea: a system never reaches directly into another system's data. It politely asks, through a defined doorway, and gets a defined answer back.

Let's make it concrete with money. Suppose System A wants to know the balance of account 123. It doesn't open System B's database. It sends a small request through the API, and gets a small answer back:

Now here's where API mapping comes in — and you'll see it's the same idea as Part 2, just applied to that little request and answer.

The two systems must agree, in writing, on exactly what each piece of the message means: "What I call the 'account number' is the same thing you call the 'account reference'. What I call the 'amount' is what you call the 'balance' — and it will always be in euros, always with the pennies shown." If you've ever worked with SWIFT, you already know this feeling: SWIFT is itself an agreed messaging channel with agreed fields. An API is the same idea, generalised to any two systems.

An API is the doorway two systems talk through. API mapping is the agreement about what every word said through that doorway means. Same mapping idea as Maria's contact card — now between live systems.

Why it's needed: Without an API, systems would have to reach into each other's databases — fragile, unsafe, impossible to control. Without API mapping, they'd talk past each other — one says "amount", the other hears "balance", and money goes wrong. The Business Analyst writes that agreement down so it can't be misunderstood.
Part 4 of 7 · The real banking example

A payment is just a form being re-copied

You now have everything you need. A bank payment is exactly like Maria's contact card — only the "boxes" hold money details, and there are a lot of them.

When a bank sends money, it fills in a structured form and sends it down the wire. There are two such forms in the world right now:

  • The old form, called SWIFT MT103. Think of it as a paper form with numbered lines: line :50: is the sender, line :59: is the receiver, line :32A: is the amount. Banks have used it for decades.
  • The new form, called ISO 20022. Same information, but laid out more clearly, with each piece given a proper name — the Debtor (the sender), the Creditor (the receiver), the Amount, and so on. Plain words instead of numbered lines.

The whole banking world is slowly switching from the old form to the new one. So someone has to write the rulebook: "the sender line of the old form becomes the Debtor on the new form; the amount drops its commas; the date flips its format…" — one plain instruction for every single field.

Look at the bottom row. The old form left :57A: (the receiver's bank) blank — that happens all the time. But the new form requires it. So the rule can't just say "copy" — there's nothing to copy.

📮 Everyday picture · the missing postcode

You're posting a parcel and the address has no postcode. You don't give up — you look up the postcode from the street and city you do have, and write it in. Now the parcel can be delivered.

Filling in a missing piece by looking it up from what you already know is called enrichment. That's the whole word.

So the rule for that last line reads: "If the receiver's bank is blank, look up the receiver's account number in our reference table, find their bank code (called a BIC), and fill it in." The job ad calls this "enrichment logic" — now you know it just means "fill in the missing box by looking it up."

Why a bank cares so much about one blank box

You might think: it's just one field. Why pay a senior person to fuss over it? Here's why — and it's the heart of Treasury & Intraday Liquidity, the team in the job title.

A bank must know, at every moment of the day, exactly how much cash it has available. Not at the end of the day — right now, minute by minute. ("Intraday" simply means "within the day.") Regulators demand the bank never let that available cash dip below a safety line.

Every payment changes that running total. If even one payment is labelled wrong — sent to the wrong category because a field was mismapped — the bank's picture of its own cash becomes wrong. And a wrong picture can look like the bank crossed the safety line when it didn't (or hide that it really did). Both are serious.

Recap of the banking part: A payment is a form. Moving from the old form to the new one is message mapping — the same idea as Maria's contact card. Enrichment means filling in a missing box by looking it up. And it all matters because one wrong box can make a bank's cash position look wrong — which regulators take very seriously.
Part 5 of 7 · JIRA

What is JIRA, and why is it needed?

We've written rules. But a real project has thousands of small jobs, and dozens of people doing them. How does anyone keep track? That's the problem JIRA solves.

📋 Everyday picture · the sticky-note board

Picture a big whiteboard with three columns: To Do, Doing, Done. Every job is a sticky note. As work progresses, you drag a note from one column to the next. Anyone walking past can see, in two seconds, what's left and who's on what.

JIRA is that board — but online, and shared by the whole team. Nothing more frightening than a very organised to-do list.

Why is it needed? Because without it, jobs live in people's heads and in scattered emails. Things get forgotten. Two people accidentally do the same task. Nobody knows if the project is on track. JIRA gives everyone one shared, always-up-to-date picture.

Part 6 of 7 · Confluence

What is Confluence, and why is it needed?

A JIRA ticket is short — a sticky note. But the full explanation behind it can be pages long. Where does that live? In Confluence.

📓 Everyday picture · the team's shared notebook

Think of a giant shared notebook — or a private Wikipedia that only your team can read and edit. Every page explains one thing in full: how a process works, why a decision was made, the complete rulebook for the payment mapping.

Confluence is that notebook. If JIRA is the short to-do note, Confluence is the full instructions the note points to.

Why is it needed? Because knowledge that lives only in someone's head leaves when they do. And a sticky note saying "fill in the bank code" isn't enough for a developer who needs the exact rule. So the ticket links to a Confluence page that holds the full story — including the mapping table from FIG 4.

As the Business Analyst, the pages you write and look after include:

The full rulebooks

The complete mapping specifications, written so a developer never has to guess what a field should do.

The decisions & notes

Why the team chose to do it this way, meeting notes, open questions — the project's memory, in one place.

The "did we miss anything?" list

A checklist (called a traceability matrix) that proves every rule we promised has actually been built and tested.

That last one is worth seeing, because it's where JIRA and Confluence shake hands. It's just a table that lines up three things: what we promised, the ticket that builds it, and the test that proves it — so a job can't quietly fall through the cracks.

Recap: Confluence = the team's shared notebook, where the full rulebooks and decisions live. JIRA tickets are short and link out to the matching Confluence page for the detail.

Bonus · How to get ready

A simple plan to prepare

Two things: learn enough of the words to sound at home, and make a couple of example pages so you can show the skill, not just talk about it.

1

Learn the "old form → new form" story

Read a short intro to the switch from SWIFT MT to ISO 20022. You don't need every detail — just be able to say why banks are moving and what the two forms are. This is the single hottest topic in payments right now.

2

Skim the old payment form

Look at what an MT103 contains (sender, receiver, amount). Half an hour is enough to read one and not be intimidated.

3

Understand "cash position" & the safety line

Just the idea from FIG 5: a bank must know its available cash all day, and must stay above a regulator's line. The rule behind that line is called BCBS 248 — knowing the name is a nice touch.

4

Click around JIRA & Confluence once

Sign up for a free Atlassian trial. Make a board, write one ticket with acceptance criteria, write one Confluence page. Now you can speak from experience, not theory.

5

Make two example pages to bring

A small mapping table (like FIG 4) and a small "did-we-miss-anything" checklist (like FIG 8). Two pages that quietly prove you can do the core of the job.

6

Tell your own version of this story

Have you ever specified how one system's data becomes another's — in any job? That is this skill. Describe it using the words from this page, and lead with it.
