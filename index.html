The job, in one line

Inside a bank, money and instructions move constantly between systems that do not speak the same language. A payment engine, a liquidity calculator, the ledger, SWIFT, reconciliation, reporting — each holds the same facts in a different shape.

The Business Analyst is the translator who writes the rulebook that says exactly how a fact in one system becomes the same fact in the next — and proves nothing was lost along the way.

Everything below — mapping specs, JIRA, Confluence — is just the toolkit for doing that translation reliably, at scale, with an audit trail a regulator will accept.

Concept 01

What "API / data / message mapping specifications" actually means

It's one skill wearing three hats. In every case you are writing a precise table that says: this field, here, becomes that field, there — under these rules.

01

Message mapping

Payments travel as structured messages. Old world: SWIFT MT (MT103, MT202). New world: ISO 20022 (pacs.008, camt.053). You map field-by-field, tag-by-tag.

02

Data mapping

The same idea for data at rest — in databases and feeds. Source column → target column, with type, length, format and a transform rule. This is where data lineage lives.

03

API mapping

When systems talk in real time, you specify the request/response contract: every attribute, mandatory or optional, its validation, and how it ties to the internal model.

Notice the bottom row. The old MT103 left the beneficiary's bank (:57A:) blank — common in real life. The new ISO message requires it. So the spec can't just say "copy." It must say: "if blank, look up the beneficiary's IBAN in the reference table, derive the BIC, and populate <CdtrAgt>." That sentence is enrichment logic — and the ad asks for it by name.

Concept 02 · the stakes

Why Treasury & Intraday Liquidity makes this high-stakes

Intraday liquidity is knowing your true cash position right now, across every account and currency. One mismapped field and the number is wrong — and a wrong number is a regulatory breach.

Controls & reconciliation

The spec defines how two systems are reconciled and where a control fires. Bad mapping = silent breaks.

Data lineage

Trace any number on a liquidity report all the way back to the originating transaction. Regulators ask for exactly this.

MI & reporting

Management information is only as trustworthy as the fields feeding it. The BA guarantees the inputs.

Tool 01 · the delivery engine

What the role needs you for in JIRA

JIRA is where the work is broken down, tracked and proven done. As the BA you turn requirements into well-formed stories with acceptance criteria — and trace every one to delivery.

A story you'd write

BIC enrichment

As a payments processor, I want a missing beneficiary BIC to be derived automatically, so that the ISO message validates without manual repair.

ACCEPTANCE CRITERIA ✓ GIVEN :57A: is blank WHEN the IBAN resolves to one BIC THEN <CdtrAgt><BIC> is populated ✓ GIVEN the IBAN resolves to none THEN route to repair queue + MI alert ✓ No payment is auto-released on a guessed BIC
links → FIG 2 spec links → QA test set links → Confluence BRD

Day to day in JIRA you would: groom the backlog, write and prioritise stories, attach crisp acceptance criteria, link each story to its test cases and defects, and walk it across the board (To Do → In Progress → In Test → Done). If the programme is Agile/SAFe, you also run refinement and sprint planning.

"High-quality requirements and acceptance criteria" in the job ad is the skill of writing good JIRA stories. That phrase and JIRA are the same thing.

Tool 02 · the knowledge home

What the role needs you for in Confluence

If JIRA is what we're doing, Confluence is what it means. It's where your specs, decisions and the traceability matrix live — the single source of truth a developer, tester or auditor opens to answer "what should this field do?"

You author

BRDs & functional specs, the mapping spec itself, process & operational design, data-flow diagrams.

You maintain

Decision logs, meeting notes, open-question registers, stakeholder sign-off pages — the project's memory.

You prove

The traceability matrix: every requirement → a story → a test → a delivered feature. Nothing dropped.

How the three fit together

One loop, three tools

The mapping spec says what's true. The JIRA story gets it built. The Confluence page explains and proves it. A developer never has to guess — the story links to the spec; the matrix links it all back.

How to prepare

A focused plan to be ready

Two tracks: learn the domain language so you sound credible in five minutes, and build artifacts so you can show the skill, not just claim it.

1

Learn the ISO 20022 migration story

The live theme in payments. Know why banks are moving MT → MX, and what pacs.008 / camt.053 are for. This is the ad's "legacy-to-strategic migration" hint, spelled out.

2

Get the SWIFT MT basics

MT103, MT202/202COV, MT940/950 — just their purpose and a few key fields (:50, :59, :32A). Enough to read a message and talk about it.

3

Understand intraday liquidity & BCBS 248

What a cash position is, nostro/vostro accounts, why timing matters, and the monitoring tools a regulator expects. This is the "why it's high-stakes" you saw in FIG 3.

4

Be fluent in JIRA & Confluence

Epic/Story/Sub-task hierarchy, writing acceptance criteria, linking issues, the traceability matrix. A free Atlassian trial + an afternoon of clicking is enough to speak from experience.

5

Build three artifacts

A real mapping spec (like FIG 2), a traceability matrix (FIG 5), and a one-page BRD. Three pages that prove the core skill — bring them to the interview.

6

Reframe your adjacent experience

Any work where you specified how one system's data becomes another's is message/data mapping. Dress it in this vocabulary and lead with it.
